What is a Mineral?

Minerals are inorganic nutrients (they don't contain carbon, unlike vitamins) that the body needs in small amounts for various essential functions.
They come from the earth and water and enter the food chain through plants and animals.
Minerals cannot be made by the human body, so they must be obtained from diet.
They are involved in building body structures (like bones and teeth), regulating processes (like nerve signaling, muscle contraction, fluid balance), and acting as cofactors for enzymes in metabolism.

Types of Major minerals

1. Calcium (Ca)

→ bones, teeth, muscle contraction, blood clotting.

2. Phosphorus (P)

→ bones, energy metabolism (ATP), DNA

3. Magnesium (Mg)

→ enzyme cofactor, nerve & muscle function

4. Sodium (Na)

→ fluid balance, nerve impulses, muscle contraction

5. Potassium (K)

→ heart rhythm, fluid balance, nerve signals

6. Chloride (Cl)

→ part of stomach acid (HCl), fluid balance

7. Sulfur (S)

→ found in amino acids (methionine, cysteine), important for proteins

Types of Microminerals

1. Iron (Fe)

→ hemoglobin in red blood cells, oxygen transport

2. Zinc (Zn)

→ immune function, wound healing, enzyme activity

3. Copper (Cu)

→ works with iron in red blood cell formation, connective tissue

4. Manganese (Mn)

→ enzyme cofactor, bone development

5. Iodine (I)

→thyroid hormone production

6. Selenium (Se)

→ antioxidant defense, thyroid function

7. Fluoride (F)

→ strengthens teeth, prevents dental cavities

8. Chromium (Cr)

→ helps regulate blood sugar by aiding insulin action

9.Molybdenum (Mo)

→cofactor for enzymes that break down amino acids and drugs
